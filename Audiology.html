Audiology

  • The anatomy and physiology of the auditory system.
  • The measurement and assessment of hearing and hearing disorders.
  • The diagnosis and treatment of hearing and balance disorders.
  • The use of audiometric testing to assess hearing loss and other hearing disorders.
  • The use of hearing aids, cochlear implants, and other assistive devices to improve hearing.
  • The effects of noise exposure on hearing.
  • The use of electrophysiological techniques (such as auditory brainstem response and otoacoustic emissions testing) to assess hearing.
  • The use of imaging techniques (such as magnetic resonance imaging or computed tomography) to study the auditory system.
  • The impact of hearing loss on cognitive development, language acquisition, and communication skills.
  • The effects of aging on hearing and hearing disorders.
  • The use of auditory training and rehabilitation to improve hearing and communication skills.
  • The use of pharmacological and surgical treatments for hearing and balance disorders.
  • The use of auditory prosthetics (such as cochlear implants) to improve hearing in individuals with severe hearing loss.
  • The use of audiologic assessment and treatment in newborns and young children.
  • The study of auditory processing disorders (disorders that affect how the brain processes auditory information).
  • The study of tinnitus (ringing in the ears) and its treatment.
  • The study of auditory neuropathy (a disorder that affects the auditory nerve and the brain's ability to process auditory information).
  • The study of auditory hallucinations (hearing voices or other sounds that are not present in the environment).
  • The study of auditory-verbal therapy (a treatment approach that uses auditory stimulation and verbal communication to improve language and communication skills).
  • The study of auditory-based approaches to cognitive rehabilitation (treatments that use auditory stimulation to improve cognitive function).
